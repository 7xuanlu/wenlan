# What AI memory benchmarks actually measure, and what they hide

By Qi-Xuan Lu

I build a local memory layer for AI agents. To know whether my retrieval is any good, I run two benchmarks over and over: LoCoMo and LongMemEval. I have stared at the per-case output of both more times than is healthy. This essay is what I learned, and what I think the whole category is quietly lying about.

The short version: these benchmarks measure whether a system retrieves the correct fact from a synthetic chat history. That is a real and useful thing to measure. It is not the thing most vendors imply when they put one big number on a landing page. The number measures "is retrieval correct." It does not measure "does anyone want this memory" or "did this help a real coding session." The gap between those two is where most of the product value lives, and no leaderboard touches it.

Let me show you the machinery, then the traps, then why I will not quote you a single number for my own tool.

## What LoCoMo actually is

LoCoMo is "Evaluating Very Long-Term Conversational Memory of LLM Agents," Maharana et al., 2024 [VERIFIED https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.17753]. It builds very long synthetic conversations between two LLM-driven personas, seeded with persona sheets and a temporally-ordered event graph, spanning up to 32 sessions and around 600 turns across simulated months [VERIFIED https://snap-research.github.io/locomo/]. Then it asks questions over that history.

The questions come in categories: single-hop (answer is in one session), multi-hop (synthesize across sessions), temporal (reason about when things happened), open-domain (combine dialogue with world knowledge), and adversarial (the answer is not in the history; the model should refuse) [VERIFIED https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.17753].

Hold onto that last category. It is where a lot of public numbers quietly break.

A high LoCoMo score proves your system can pull a stated fact back out of a long synthetic transcript and that a judge model agreed with the phrasing. That is the whole claim. It does not prove the conversations resemble how anyone talks to a real assistant; they were generated by LLMs from event graphs. It does not prove the answer key is correct. An independent audit found 99 score-corrupting errors in 1,540 questions, about 6.4 percent, including hallucinated facts in the answer key, bad temporal reasoning, and speaker-attribution errors [VERIFIED https://dev.to/penfieldlabs/we-audited-locomo-64-of-the-answer-key-is-wrong-and-the-judge-accepts-up-to-63-of-intentionally-33lg]. When the ruler has a 6 percent error bar baked in, any two systems within 6 percent of each other are tied, and most published gaps are smaller than that. [INFERRED]

## What LongMemEval actually is

LongMemEval is the ICLR 2025 benchmark "Benchmarking Chat Assistants on Long-Term Interactive Memory," Wu et al. [VERIFIED https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.10813]. It is more carefully built than LoCoMo. 500 hand-curated questions embedded in scalable user-assistant chat histories, targeting five abilities: information extraction, multi-session reasoning, temporal reasoning, knowledge updates, and abstention [VERIFIED https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.10813].

Two of those five are the interesting ones, because they are the ones marketing forgets. Knowledge updates tests whether your system notices that a fact changed (the user moved cities, switched jobs) and stops returning the stale version. Abstention tests whether the system refrains from answering when the information was never in the history [VERIFIED https://xiaowu0162.github.io/long-mem-eval/]. Abstention is the one that separates a memory system from a confabulation engine, and it is the one a single headline accuracy number averages into invisibility. [OPINION]

LongMemEval's own headline is honest about difficulty: long-context LLMs show a 30 to 60 percent accuracy drop on the harder split, and strong commercial systems land in the 30 to 70 percent range in an easier setting [VERIFIED https://arxiv.org/html/2410.10813v2]. That is the real shape of the problem. Memory over long histories is not close to solved. Any vendor number that reads like it is solved should make you suspicious of the methodology. [OPINION]

## Trap one: the single number

Walk into any memory-startup landing page and you will find one figure. Mem0, for example, reports a 26 percent relative improvement over OpenAI's memory on LoCoMo, 66.9 percent versus 52.9 percent on an LLM-as-judge score [VERIFIED https://mem0.ai/research-3]. I am not picking on Mem0 specifically; this is the genre. The figure is presented as a property of the product. It is actually a property of one run, one configuration, one baseline, one judge model, on one benchmark with a 6 percent answer-key error rate. [INFERRED]

Here is my rule, written into my own repo before I ever published a number:

> "Single-run rule. Any baseline with env.is_single_run = true MUST NOT be cited externally. Internal team references are fine but must be flagged 'single-run, treat as scaffold.' Full citation requires the P1.5 multi-run protocol (mean plus or minus stddev over at least 3 runs, ideally 10)." [from AGENTS.md, Eval Citation Discipline]

It is enforced in code, not vibes. Every baseline my harness writes stamps an `is_single_run` flag:

> "is_single_run: always true for single-run tests. Any baseline with is_single_run = true must not be cited externally." [from crates/origin-core/src/eval/AGENTS.md]

The reason is boring and decisive: LLM judges are nondeterministic, retrieval has tie-breaking noise, and one run tells you nothing about variance. If you cannot show me a standard deviation, you have not shown me a result. You have shown me an anecdote with a decimal point. [OPINION]


## Trap two: comparing across schema versions

This one is subtle and everywhere. A vendor improves their system, changes how memories are stored or chunked or scored, reruns the benchmark, and reports the new number against the old. The two numbers were produced by different pipelines. The comparison is meaningless, but it looks like progress.

My harness refuses to do this. It stamps every baseline with an environment hash over fixture revision, embedder revision, LLM provider class, model, schema version, and similarity function [from app/eval/AGENTS.md]. The rule:

> "Schema-version rule. Cross-env.schema_version comparisons are refused by compare-baselines (exit code 2). Public claims that compare numbers across schema versions MUST regenerate both sides via current save_*_baseline tests." [from AGENTS.md, Eval Citation Discipline]

The tool exits with an error code rather than printing a tempting delta. I built it that way because I caught myself wanting to quote a flattering before/after that the machinery would not actually stand behind. The honest move is to regenerate both sides under one pipeline. It is slower, and it is the only version that is true. [OPINION]

## Trap three: the adversarial category hides the regression

This is my favorite trap because it is so mechanical and so common.

LoCoMo's adversarial category is category 5: questions whose answer is not in the history, where a good system should refuse. Of LoCoMo's roughly 1,986 QA pairs, 446 are category 5, and the standard convention is to report on the 1,540 non-adversarial subset, because category 5 has documented ground-truth problems and tests refusal rather than recall [VERIFIED https://github.com/MemPalace/mempalace/issues/29]. Include those 446 by accident and your headline number shifts for reasons that have nothing to do with memory quality. [INFERRED]

This is not hypothetical, and the honest version of the story is messier than either vendor wants. Zep first reported 84 percent on LoCoMo. A re-audit by Mem0, applying corrected category handling, recomputed it at 58.44 percent [VERIFIED https://github.com/getzep/zep-papers/issues/5]. Zep then acknowledged a calculation error of their own and published a corrected 75.14 percent plus or minus 0.17 [VERIFIED https://blog.getzep.com/lies-damn-lies-statistics-is-mem0-really-sota-in-agent-memory/]. So one benchmark, one system, produced three different headline numbers depending on who computed it and how: 84, 75.14, 58.44. I am deliberately not telling you which is right, because the point is not which vendor wins. The point is that the spread between the lowest and highest figure for the same system is about 25 absolute points, larger than almost every "we beat them by X percent" gap anyone publishes. When a single system's score moves 25 points on methodology alone, every cross-vendor comparison in the category is noise until you control the methodology. And note the trap I almost fell into writing this paragraph: quoting only 84 to 58.44 would have been its own cherry-pick, the Mem0-favorable cut of a contested number. [INFERRED]

In my repo I named this trap by hand so I would never forget it:

> "Per-case visibility. Aggregate accuracy claims must include per-case breakdown when available. Headline-only numbers hide regressions (LoCoMo adversarial-cat-5 contamination is the canonical trap)." [from AGENTS.md, Eval Citation Discipline]

The discipline is simple: never report an aggregate without the per-category breakdown beside it. If a system gets better at single-hop recall and worse at abstention, the average can stay flat or even rise while the product gets worse, because it now confidently makes things up. The average is exactly the wrong summary statistic for a memory system, and it is the one everyone publishes. [OPINION]

## Trap four: no receipt

The last trap is claims without measurement behind them. "Improved retrieval by 15 percent." Against what baseline? How many runs? What variance? What did it cost to produce? My rule:

> "Receipt-only rule. Regression thresholds, latency claims, accuracy improvements must have a measured-stddev or N greater than or equal to 3-run backing. No 'improved X percent' or 'regressed Y percent' without compare-baselines output AND multi-run inputs." [from AGENTS.md, Eval Citation Discipline]

And one more, because cross-layer averaging is its own quiet lie:

> "Layer attribution. Public numbers must specify L1 / L2 / L3 / L4. No cross-layer averages without explicit weighting." [from AGENTS.md, Eval Citation Discipline]

My test stack runs in layers L1 through L8: the IDE loop, pre-commit, pre-push, CI on PR, coverage, a main-branch canary, manual GPU evals on my laptop, and a pre-release full suite [from AGENTS.md, Local vs CI test responsibilities]. The GPU evals, the ones that produce the headline retrieval numbers, never run in CI, because hosted runners have no Metal acceleration and the tests are marked ignore so they do not silently run [from AGENTS.md]. A number from L7 on my laptop and a number from L4 in CI are not the same kind of evidence. Quoting one as if it were the other is how layer attribution gets lost. [INFERRED]

## The thing none of them measure

Now the real point. Every benchmark above measures the same axis: given a question with a known answer somewhere in a synthetic history, did the system retrieve it. Even at their best, even fully de-trapped, they answer one question: is retrieval correct.

They do not measure whether the memory was worth storing, whether a human wanted it kept, or whether surfacing it mid-task helped or distracted. A system can score beautifully on LoCoMo while filling a user's memory with trivia nobody asked it to remember, because the benchmark hands it the questions in advance. Real memory has no answer key. The hard problem is deciding what to keep and what to forget, and no QA benchmark can score that, because the questions are given. [OPINION]

This is why I built faithfulness benches that the QA scores cannot see. One scores knowledge-graph extraction against hand-curated entity and relation ground truth. One scores distilled summary pages by checking what fraction of sentences have real token overlap with their source memories, with deliberate hallucination fixtures to confirm the scorer flags invented claims [from AGENTS.md, KG-faithfulness and page-faithfulness benches]. These are lexical and crude, and I document them as crude. But they ask a different question than LoCoMo: not "did you find the fact" but "did you make something up." For a memory system the second question matters more, and the famous leaderboards do not ask it. [OPINION]

## The pitch: run it yourself

Here is the part I actually care about. All of this is reproducible, and none of it is behind a wall.

The benchmarks are public. LoCoMo is on GitHub from Snap Research [VERIFIED https://github.com/snap-research/locomo]. LongMemEval is on GitHub and Hugging Face [VERIFIED https://github.com/xiaowu0162/LongMemEval]. My harness drops the fixtures into a known path and runs:

```
cargo test -p origin-core --test eval_harness save_locomo_baseline -- --ignored --nocapture
cargo test -p origin-core --test eval_harness save_longmemeval_baseline -- --ignored --nocapture
```

Set `EVAL_LOCOMO_LIMIT=20` to do a roughly 30-minute subset before committing to a multi-hour full run [from app/eval/AGENTS.md, smoke vs full run sizing]. Every baseline lands stamped with its environment hash and its `is_single_run` flag, so you can see exactly what it is and is not allowed to claim.

So when you ask me for my LoCoMo number, here is my answer: not yet. I have single-run scaffold numbers on my laptop. My own rules forbid me from quoting them to you, because one run with no standard deviation is an anecdote, and I wrote that rule down before I had a number I liked. When I have run the full fixture at least three times, reported the mean and standard deviation, shown the per-category breakdown including abstention, and stamped the pipeline version, I will publish that. Until then the honest number is silence.

If a vendor will not show you the variance, the per-category split, and the pipeline version, they are not showing you a benchmark result. They are showing you a billboard. Ask for the receipt.
